Phototherapeutic Device Engineering

This page summarizes my work on phototherapeutic device engineering. The overall goal is to design wearable/portable light-based therapeutic systems that deliver controlled spectral power to the skin/retina while maintaining safety and comfort.

Sub-Projects

  • Photon-Transport Simulation for Phototherapeutic Device Diffusion Layer
  • Clinical Effect of Blue-Suppressed Light Source

Photon-Transport Simulation for Phototherapeutic Device Diffusion Layer

In this project, I develop a Monte Carlo-based photon-tracing simulation model to study light transport in the diffusion layer of wearable phototherapeutic devices. The diffusion layer leverages hollow nanoparticles as engineered scatterers to improve uniformity at the skin surface and mitigate local hot spots that could cause discomfort or adverse effects.

The simulation framework is grounded in Mie theory, which provide the scattering properties of the hollow nanoparticles embedded in a host medium. These optical properties are then integrated into a Monte Carlo photon-transport model to:

  • predict spatial irradiance distributions at the device-skin interface,
  • evaluate the impact of particle size, shell thickness, and concentration,
  • optimize the diffusion layer design for both efficiency and safety.

Clinical Effect of Blue-Suppressed Light Source

This project aimed to show the physiological and psychological impact of blue-suppressed light sources, motivated by concerns about circadian disruption, stress, and sleep quality associated with conventional blue-rich lighting.

A blue-suppressed light device with a tailored spectral profile was designed to reduce short-wavelength emissions while maintaining sufficient visual brightness and usability. Based on this hardware platform, I contributed in the design of experimental protocol for clinical studies aimed at observing:

  • hormonal changes related to circadian and stress regulation,
  • self-reported and physiological indicators of stress levels,
  • objective and subjective measures of sleep quality.

By combining device engineering with clinical assessment, this work seeks to clarify how spectral tuning can be used to create health-conscious lighting solutions that better align with human physiology while remaining practical for everyday use.
